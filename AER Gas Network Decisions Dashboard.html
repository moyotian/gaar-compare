The depreciation building block comprises two components: (1) Standard regulatory depreciation — return of capital calculated using standard (straight-line) asset lives applied to the approved capital base; and (2) Accelerated depreciation — an additional approved amount to address stranded asset risk by returning capital to investors faster, at the cost of higher near-term prices for customers. Accelerated depreciation is determined under rule 89 of the National Gas Rules.

Key Commentary
JGN 2025–30 — $115M approved (vs $230M proposed): The AER approved $115M accelerated depreciation, halving JGN's $230M proposal. Of the approved $115M: $77M comes from shortened economic lives for certain asset classes; $38M comes from a reduction in the capital base. The AER considered $115M strikes an appropriate balance — providing a meaningful start to managing stranded asset risk while avoiding unnecessarily large price increases. The AER expects to revisit the appropriate level in future periods as demand trajectory becomes clearer. Overview pp. 7, 24–26
Evoenergy 2026–31 — $30M approved (vs $65M proposed): The AER approved $30M through shortened asset lives for HP and MP pipeline assets (to 19 years, aligned to ACT Government's 2045 gas network decommissioning target under the Integrated Energy Plan). An additional $35M proposed by Evoenergy beyond the reduced-asset-lives approach was not accepted — the AER found that reduced asset lives already addressed residual stranded asset risk materially, and that further accelerated depreciation would cause additional upward price pressure (~8.6% p.a. real network price increase) without sufficient justification. Overview pp. 5–6, 24–27
AGN SA 2021–26 — $250M approved: The largest single accelerated depreciation amount in this comparison, approved to recover the net book value of replaced mains and inlets from the major SA mains replacement program. This reflects the retirement of assets from service — a clear trigger for accelerated depreciation. The approved amount was lower than AGN's initial proposal, reflecting the AER's assessment of the quantum of eligible mains replacement and corrections to input errors in AGN's depreciation model. Overview pp. 34–36
AGN SA 2026–31 — $29M approved (vs $70M proposed): A dramatic fall from $250M in 2021–26, with the mains replacement program substantially complete. The $29M approved reflects only reduced asset lives for the Mains and Inlets asset classes (to 30 and 50 years respectively). AGN proposed $70M, arguing this was the minimum viable amount; the AER did not accept this, finding the reduced asset lives approach sufficient to address residual stranded asset risk at lower cost to consumers. Overview pp. 7–8, 22–24
AusNet 2023–28 — $105M approved (vs $200M proposed): The AER described this as a "measured start" to accelerated depreciation for AusNet's Victorian distribution network. The $105M reflects a proportionate response to stranded asset risk while maintaining price affordability for consumers. AusNet's $200M proposal was found to be larger than necessary at this stage, given uncertainty about the pace of gas demand decline. The AER noted it would monitor demand trajectory and revisit in future reviews. Overview pp. 6–7, 22–24
Multinet 2023–28 — $53M approved (vs $86M proposed): Similarly described as a "measured start" to accelerated depreciation for Multinet's aging Melbourne network. The $53M balances addressing stranded asset risk against price impacts for remaining customers. The AER's reduction ($33M from MGN's proposal) reflects the same approach applied to AusNet — deferring a larger step-change in accelerated depreciation until the demand trajectory is clearer. Overview pp. 6–7, 22–24
AGN Vic 2023–28 — $175M approved (as proposed): The largest accelerated depreciation approved in the current Victorian round, and the only Victorian decision where the proposal was accepted in full (the draft decision also accepted $175M). This reflects AGN's extensive Victorian network and the assessed stranded asset risk level. The approval without reduction (unlike AusNet and Multinet) may reflect AGN's stronger supporting evidence base for the proposed amount. Overview pp. 6, 22–24

Customer Numbers & Gas Delivered — Key findings (AER Network Performance Report 2026)

Customer connections: Net residential connections grew 1.5% across the sector in 2025. AGN Vic recorded the strongest growth at +3%. Multinet's customer base was broadly flat. The sector continues to add customers in aggregate, but growth rates have slowed markedly compared to earlier years.
Evoenergy declining: Evoenergy's customer numbers have fallen for the third consecutive year, following the ACT Government's ban on new gas connections introduced in December 2023. This is the first regulatory intervention of its kind in Australia to directly reduce gas customer growth. Evoenergy is the only network to face a structural customer decline as of 2025.
Victorian electrification policy: The Victorian Government has introduced electrification regulations (June 2025) requiring all-electric new buildings from 1 January 2027 and mandating gas hot water replacement at end-of-life from 1 March 2027. The AER notes that the impact of these policies is not expected to appear in regulatory reporting until the 2027 regulatory year, but they represent a material structural risk to future customer growth for AGN Vic, AusNet and Multinet.
Gas delivered declining: Aggregate gas delivered fell 3% in 2025, continuing a multi-year trend. JGN was the only network to record a slight increase. Evoenergy, AGN Vic, AGN SA and Multinet all fell more than 5%. Contributing factors include milder temperatures in 2025 (particularly in Victoria), ongoing electrification of appliances, improved appliance efficiency and cost-of-living pressures. Notably, the historical correlation between effective degree days (EDD) and gas demand has been weakening — suggesting structural drivers are increasingly dominant over weather.

Per Customer Metrics — Key findings (AER Network Performance Report 2026)

Revenue per customer: Average revenue per customer across the sector was $365 in 2025, up $10 in real terms from 2024. The level and trajectory vary significantly between networks, reflecting differences in access arrangement allowances, network scale, tariff structures and the degree to which actual revenue tracks target revenue. Under weighted average price cap regulation, revenue per customer will fall if customer numbers grow faster than the total approved revenue — or if actual revenues fall below target due to declining gas throughput.
Opex per customer: Opex per customer reflects both scale efficiency and the nature of each network's obligations. Networks with large mains replacement or asset renewal programs tend to carry higher opex per customer in the near term. The sustained sector-wide underspend relative to AER allowances means actual opex per customer has generally tracked below what regulators expected when setting access arrangements.
Cross-network comparison: JGN and AGN SA tend to have lower opex per customer than smaller networks, reflecting scale benefits from larger customer bases. Multinet and AusNet sit in a mid-range. Evoenergy — despite being the smallest network — benefits from its all-polyethylene infrastructure (no metallic mains replacement) and a concentrated urban service area (ACT), resulting in relatively manageable per-customer costs. However, declining customer numbers in Evoenergy's network may push per-customer costs upward as fixed costs are spread across a shrinking base.

Revenue — Key findings (AER Network Performance Report 2026)

Aggregate 2025: DNSPs collectively recovered $1.7 billion in revenue in 2025 — up $63M (4%) from 2024. Average revenue per customer was $365, an increase of $10 in real terms. Despite this, aggregate revenue remains around 17% below the 2015 peak, reflecting structural demand decline and falling access arrangement allowances over time.
Actual vs target: All DNSPs except AusNet earned below their target revenue in 2025. AusNet was the only network to marginally exceed its target (+0.4%). This is a direct consequence of the weighted average price cap regime — under which demand risk sits entirely with the DNSP, not customers. Gas delivered across the sector fell ~3% in 2025, compressing actual revenues below approved targets.
JGN (+14%): JGN recorded the largest revenue increase, driven by a tariff reconciliation for unaccounted-for-gas (UAFG) costs arising from elevated gas price volatility in 2022–23. This was a pass-through mechanism rather than a reflection of increased demand or pricing.

Operating Expenditure — Key findings (AER Network Performance Report 2026)

Aggregate 2025: Total opex across all six DNSPs was $614M in 2025, up 2% from 2024. For the eighth consecutive year, the sector collectively spent below its AER-approved allowance — with a cumulative $543M (11%) underspend since 2018. This sustained efficiency trend reflects both effective cost management and the incentive properties of the regulatory framework, where networks retain a share of any savings relative to allowances.
Recent overspends: In the last five years, only two exceptions occurred: AGN Vic marginally overspent in 2021, and JGN recorded an opex overspend in 2024 driven by elevated UAFG gas purchase costs (a cost pass-through item tied to gas price spikes). No DNSP recorded a significant structural opex overspend across the period.
Incentive effects: The incentive framework means that sustained efficiency gains reduce future approved allowances — regulators use actual expenditure as a benchmark for setting forecasts in subsequent access arrangements. This creates a ratchet-like dynamic where continued underspending raises the bar for future periods.

Capital Expenditure — Key findings (AER Network Performance Report 2026)

Aggregate 2025: Total capex across all networks was $628M in 2025 — up 10% from 2024, but still approximately 10% below the AER-approved allowance. The increase was primarily driven by greater mains replacement activity and higher ICT spend; new customer connections fell slightly year-on-year.
Mains replacement programs: AGN SA, AusNet and Multinet are still in the process of completing significant mains replacement programs — replacing aged metallic pipes with polyethylene. These programs drive a large share of capex for those networks and are a key determinant of their relative capex profiles.
Evoenergy: Evoenergy has no mains replacement program. Its network was built entirely of polyethylene from 1982, meaning it does not face the same legacy asset renewal obligations as the Victorian and South Australian networks. Evoenergy's capex is consequently lower and more stable relative to its network size.

Capital Asset Base & UAFG — Key findings (AER Network Performance Report 2026)

CAB growth: The aggregate CAB across all six networks grew by 1.4% in real terms in 2025, reaching approximately $12.3 billion. CAB per customer remained broadly stable, but CAB per GJ delivered rose $2 to $47/GJ — up $15/GJ since 2011. The divergence between asset base growth and declining gas throughput is putting sustained upward pressure on efficiency metrics and future pricing.
AGN SA standout: AGN SA has the highest CAB/GJ delivered of any network at $115/GJ — nearly double the next highest — and has increased by 99% since 2011. This reflects a combination of continued CAB growth (driven by mains replacement) alongside a ~40% fall in industrial gas delivered, which significantly reduces the denominator. This dynamic is a key driver of AGN SA's revenue per customer trajectory.
UAFG — overall improvement: The sector-wide UAFG loss rate improved to 3.3% in 2025, from 3.6% in 2024. Victorian networks (particularly Multinet) record higher UAFG loss rates than other networks. UAFG arises from a combination of leakage, metering errors, differences in gas heating values, data quality issues, and in rare cases, theft.
Evoenergy anomaly: Evoenergy reported a negative UAFG figure in 2025, which the AER attributes to measurement or calculation error rather than a genuine gas surplus. Negative UAFG is physically implausible and should be treated as a data quality artefact.
